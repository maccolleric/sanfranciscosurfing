A History of Surfing in SF

From the first rides to a world-class surf culture

Back to Home

Ocean Beach has been attracting surfers for over a century. The story of surfing in San Francisco is one of cold water, raw determination, and a deep love for one of the most unforgiving stretches of coastline in North America.

Ocean Beach looking north toward the Cliff House, early 1900s

The Early Days: 1900s–1940s

Surfing arrived in California in the early 20th century, introduced largely by Hawaiian watermen and popularized through exhibitions and beach culture in Southern California. But San Francisco was cold, foggy, and far from the gentle warm-water breaks of Waikiki — and it drew a different kind of surfer.

The earliest documented surf activity in the Bay Area dates to the 1910s and 1920s, when a handful of adventurous swimmers and bodysurfers began riding the shorebreak at Ocean Beach. These weren't the graceful longboard rides of Hawaii — this was raw survival surfing in 54-degree water, with no wetsuits and heavy wooden boards if they had boards at all.

By the 1930s, a small but dedicated crew had taken up surfing at OB. The waves were powerful and inconsistent, the water brutally cold, and equipment primitive. But the culture was forming — a tight-knit community defined by the very harshness of its home break.

Left: Early surfers at Kelly's Cove, circa 1930s. Right: Wooden boards on the Great Highway.

The Longboard Era: 1950s–1960s

The postwar boom brought surfing to a new generation. Across California, surf culture exploded — boards got lighter, movies romanticized the lifestyle, and beach towns became meccas. San Francisco was never going to be Malibu, but OB developed its own proud identity.

The Kelly's Cove area at the north end of Ocean Beach became the heart of the SF surf scene. A core group of dedicated locals surfed it year-round, developing an ethos that prized toughness, local knowledge, and a certain disdain for the sunshine-and-palm-trees image of surfing that was being sold to the rest of America.

The invention of the neoprene wetsuit in the early 1950s was transformative for cold-water surfing everywhere. Jack O'Neill, who would go on to found O'Neill Surf Shop — first in San Francisco, then in Santa Cruz — was experimenting with wetsuit materials in this era, driven directly by the need to surf in Northern California waters without going hypothermic.

Longboarders at Kelly's Cove, late 1950s

Jack O'Neill and the Wetsuit Revolution

No figure looms larger over the history of Northern California surfing than Jack O'Neill. In the early 1950s, O'Neill was surfing Ocean Beach and experimenting with ways to stay warm in the frigid Pacific. He began testing neoprene foam — a material originally developed for military applications — as insulation against the cold.

In 1952, O'Neill opened a surf shop in the Great Highway — literally on Ocean Beach — out of his garage. He sold boards, gave lessons, and continued refining his wetsuit designs. The shop moved around over the years, eventually relocating to Santa Cruz, but O'Neill's roots were firmly planted at OB.

The wetsuit didn't just make surfing in cold water more comfortable — it made it possible for ordinary people. Before neoprene, surfing in San Francisco was a feat of endurance limited to the most committed. After it, the door opened to anyone willing to brave the cold, the fog, and the powerful swells of the Northern California coast.

The Shortboard Revolution: 1970s–1980s

The late 1960s shortboard revolution hit San Francisco like it hit everywhere — boards got shorter, lighter, and more radical. But OB's heavy, powerful beachbreak was actually well-suited to the new equipment. Shorter boards allowed surfers to deal with the steep, fast waves at Sloat and the punchy peaks up and down the beach.

The 1970s and 80s saw OB's reputation grow — it was known as one of the heaviest beach breaks in the country, a place that sorted out who was serious and who wasn't. The local crew was tight and protective of their waves, and sessions at OB became legendary for the combination of quality surf and raw, unforgiving conditions.

Fort Point also came into its own during this era. The right-hand reef break beneath the Golden Gate Bridge became a rite of passage for big-wave surfers, working only on the largest NW swells when Ocean Beach was too dangerous to paddle out.

Aerial view of Ocean Beach during a large northwest swell, 1980s

Big Waves and Growing Recognition: 1990s–2000s

By the 1990s, Ocean Beach was getting wider recognition as a legitimate big-wave destination. On the right swell, OB could produce massive walls of water that attracted some of the best surfers in the world. The break began appearing in surf publications, and the local crew found their home break thrust into the spotlight.

The Mavericks big-wave break at Half Moon Bay, about 25 miles south of San Francisco, emerged in the early 1990s as one of the most fearsome waves on the planet. While Mavericks drew the headlines, OB remained the daily proving ground — the place where Bay Area surfers built the skills and conditioning to tackle those giant swells.

The growth of surf media and the internet in the 2000s brought more attention to NorCal surfing. Video footage of massive Ocean Beach sessions circulated online, and the spot's reputation as a world-class, if brutally challenging, surf break was firmly established.

The Modern Era: 2010s–Present

Today, San Francisco has a thriving surf community spread across multiple breaks — Ocean Beach, Kelly's Cove, Fort Point, Rodeo Beach in the Marin Headlands, and Lindamar in Pacifica. The scene is more diverse and accessible than ever, thanks to better equipment, surf schools, and a growing awareness of surfing as a year-round activity for Bay Area residents.

Organizations like the City Surf Project and the MeWater Foundation have worked to make surfing accessible to communities that historically had little connection to the ocean, introducing young people from across the city to the water and building a new generation of SF surfers.

Ocean Beach remains as challenging as ever — still cold, still powerful, still capable of humbling the most experienced surfers on the planet. The Great Highway seawall, debates over coastal erosion and beach access, and the ever-present question of how to manage one of San Francisco's most beloved natural spaces continue to shape the break's future.

But the soul of the place hasn't changed. Every morning, in fog or sunshine, a crew of dedicated surfers pulls on their wetsuits, grabs their boards, and paddles out into the deep grey Pacific. The tradition continues.
